The Meissner Effect Demonstration

The YBCO superconductor disc is cooled with liquid nitrogen, then placed above the neodymium magnetic track — where it levitates, rotating slowly and silently. This single moment encapsulates quantum mechanics in a way no textbook can.

1

Cool the YBCO disc in liquid nitrogen for 60 seconds until it reaches 93 K (−180°C).

2

Assemble the 4-piece neodymium magnetic track on a flat surface.

3

Place the disc above the track — it levitates and rotates through quantum flux pinning.

4

Use the teaching guide to walk students through the physics behind what they just witnessed.

Our Mission

Why this kit exists, and the story behind it.

Brand Biography

It started the moment I realized that hands-on demonstrations dramatically improve retention and understanding of abstract concepts. I was lying down, reading the Feynman Lectures on Physics, struggling to truly grasp what I was reading. When I discovered that quantum levitation — one of the most visually striking consequences of superconductivity — was theoretically achievable with accessible materials, the idea was immediate: a kit that makes these advanced topics tangible for any student, anywhere.

What interested me most was not the superconductor itself, but the question: why does something this remarkable remain invisible to most students? The answer — cost, complexity, and a lack of well-designed kits — became the design brief for everything that followed.

My goal was simple: come up with something that can sit on a teacher's desk anywhere in the world, require no specialist knowledge to operate, and still make a student's jaw drop. The Quantum Edu Kit is that thing.

References & Sources

Research backing our educational approach

Evaluation of Research Process & Results
What the Research Confirmed

All three primary sources — the student survey, UCR interview, and expert interview — independently confirmed the same core finding: financial cost and teacher expertise are the two main barriers blocking hands-on superconductivity education. This convergence across varied sources strengthens the validity of the problem statement and the design choices that followed.

How It Shaped the Design

The expert interview directly influenced the kit's scope: step-by-step assembly instructions were added to the manual after the professor noted that teachers without prior laboratory experience would not know how to independently configure the equipment. The peer feedback led to revising the solution description to explain how the kit improves understanding, not just that it does.

Limitations

The student survey had only 11 respondents, all from the same school, which limits how broadly the results can be applied. A larger sample across multiple institutions — particularly in developing nations — would produce more reliable data. The interviews also relied on self-reported opinion rather than controlled observation.

Next Steps

Future research should test a working prototype with actual students and measure retention against a control group taught only with theory. Gathering feedback from teachers in Costa Rica and other Central American countries would give data that is directly relevant to the target audience.
